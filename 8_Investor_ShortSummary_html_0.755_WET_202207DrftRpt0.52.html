Ground-dwelling mammals in Western Port Woodlands

A camera trapping suvery 2020-2022

Author

D G Nicholls, T Coates, M Legg, A O Nicholls

Published

October 26, 2022

1 2.

Introduction

In collaboration with the Southern Brown Bandicoot Regional Recovery Group camera traps have been deployed at more than 1000 sites throughout the Western Port region to gain a better understanding of the status and distribution of target species.

This new work complements recent surveys by Parks Victoria, Bass Coast Landcare and Southern Brown Bandicoot Regional Recovery Group that have informed conservation management of the Southern Brown Bandicoot in The Bass Coast region. The local Bass Coast environmental community were concerned at the loss of biodiversity values as the sand mining industry expanded and as the former “Holden Proving Ground” (HPG) at Lang Lang was put up for private sale. This property comprises the largest local fragment of intact bushland remaining in the Bass Coast Region. Existing planning overlays provided limited protection for biodiversity at the site. The South Gippsland Conservation Society supported additional surveys, initially on public land surrounding the HPG. In response to these concerns, the society proposed a further survey of bushland throughout the Bass Coast region.

The Wettenhall Environmental Trust project, Camera trap survey of Bass Coast bushlands, public and private for Southern Brown Bandicoots, enabled the expanded survey. This project considerably enhances other, on-going camera trapping auspiced by the Southern Brown Bandicoot Regional Recovery Group. A grant from the South Gippsland Conservation Society financed a survey around the perimeter of the HPG. Concurrent with this study, the community conservation organisation Save Western Port Woodlands and Victorian National Parks Association produced a review of regional biodiversity (Logan et al. 2021). This gave a greater focus and sense of urgency for the current study. The coincidence of the objectives and the integrated nature of the trapping program, led to these projects being managed and reported as a single entity. The combined surveys are here referred to as the WET project. This note presents the results from this work.

It is intended that the results from the current surveys will be compared with the entire Western Port collection of photographs. The reporting statistics can be either the number of images or the number of detection days. The cameras primarily target ground dwelling mammals with occasional, incidental “captures” of birds and reptiles. We report the trapping effort (number of cameras and number of camera days), the total number of captures and number of mammalian species captured for each camera deployed for each species for each station.

Methods

Study area and field techniques

The study area comprised patches of remnant vegetation in the Bass Coast hinterland on the eastern side of Western Port Bay; extending from Nyora in the north, to Grantville in the south. The study area was partitioned into six discreet blocks of native vegetation, hereafter referred to as “localities” Figure 2. These were:

  • Nyora, Wuchatsch Reserve and surrounds including Cherrytree Road;
  • Adams Creek NCR, including land parcels, here called West, North, East, South and the Crown Land blocks;
  • Lang Lang-Woodleigh, private properties of Proving Ground, Flanders Field and Boral Peacock Rd and the south eastern portion of the public land of the Lang Lang Education Area;
  • The Gurdies NCR and the Bass Coast Shire Offset Reserve;
  • Granville, a Trust for Nature private property
  • Grantville NCR and a neighbouring Trust for Nature property.

Dataset

The observations were managed in an R software environment (R Core Team, 2020). The WET project, includes all vertebrate species except humans, dogs and agricultural stock. The Species data frame comprises records of images with an identified vertebrate. The Stations data frame comprises the location and duration of all camera deployments.

We use the number of images here as an indication of abundance. Alternatives are available. Examples include the number of independent images or the number of detections per calendar day. Detection-days can be derived from the R function camtrapR::detectionHistory.

Deployment of cameras

All deployments used Reconyx HC600 passive infra-red trail cameras mounted on trees and aimed slightly downward to target an area of ground 0.25-2.5 m from the camera. Cameras were set to the highest sensitivity and programmed to capture ten images at one per second when triggered by a warm or moving object. The camera model and the field settings have remained consistent throughout this study, and closely follow methods described in Nicholls et al. (2018) for details.

A station is a geographically referenced site where a camera or cameras were deployed. There may be a single, “one-off” deployment or there may be a series of deployments at the same site, each identified as a separate deployment. At such sequential deployments, the camera is serviced with a new memory card and fresh batteries. Such stations were serviced approximately every two months. The actual deployment duration (DplyDrn) is given in days. If the camera is known to be faulty the non-operational days, recorded as ‘Problem from’ to ‘Problem to’ are subtracted to give a valid deployment duration. No lures were used.

Results

Stations and deployments

There were 310 camera deployments, at 273 stations, that is sites, totalling 20538 deployment(camera)-days, see details in Appendix Table 8. The first deployment commenced on 2020-03-24 and the last ended on 2022-05-26, at the time of this report was compiled. The last camera retrieved was on 2022-04-21.

Species richness

Species richness represents the number of species recorded at a station (or station deployment). Species richness ranged from zero to ten per site with four the most commonly observed number of taxa, Figure 3.

The stations with very few target species recorded were typically stations that ran for a short period (either because there were deployment failures or the deployment was intentionally foreshortened) and therefore captured very few images, see Appendix for details, Table 9. There were 42 stations with no target species. This includes 14 stations where the images were lost; and two stations where there was equipment failure, leaving 27 stations where there were no mammals detected.

Naive occupancy

Naive occupancy is the proportion of stations where a species was recorded and assumes that if a species was not recorded it was not present. There is no attempt to estimate the probability of occupancy at a station where detection did not occur but might have if detection were improved or the camera deployed for a longer period. This refinement is achieved with a more sophisticated occupancy analysis, a task that is currently in progress.

Cameras were deployed in 6 discreet/disjunct locations of remnant native vegetation, Figure 2. The largest blocks of native vegetation (Adams Creek, and Lang Lang) had the largest number of native mammal taxa, namely ten or more species. At The Gurdies NCR (and the contiguous Shire Reserve) nine native mammals were detected. At the smallest isolated block, Nyora, seven native mammals were detected.

The species list for the six localities surveyed are presented in Table 1. There was a suite of mammals that were common throughout the entire study area. These included the Short-beaked Echidna, Agile Antechinus, Common and Mountain Brushtail Possums, Common Wombat, Black Wallaby and Bush Rat.

Adams Creek NCR and the adjacent Crown Land block is a large bushland fragment recorded 11 native species, including the Southern Brown Bandicoot. Lang Lang includes the Lang Lang Proving Ground and neighbouring private properties with the Lang Lang Education Area (Bells Park) that adjoin the Proving Ground. There were no confirmed Southern Brown Bandicoot detected but the Long-nosed Bandicoot and the Sugar Glider (Krefft’s Glider) were detected. A single detection of a bandicoot could not be identified to species level. The Gurdies NCR and the Shire Reserve (to its north) had the common mammals. At the Grantville private property (only four stations), the macropods dominated with the Common Brushtail Possum replacing the Mountain Brushtail Possum. At the Grantville NCR locality, which included the Grantville NCR, Transfer Station, Adams Estate (both north of the reserve), and the adjacent Trust for Nature property to the east of the reserve, both Brushtail Possums were detected with the Koala and the common mammals. Dusky Antechinus was detected at only one site (Wuchatsch Reserve, Nyora). This site is a very steep gully with tall forest and wet under storey.

For the introduced mammals, the Black Rat and the European Rabbit were detected at four of the six localities. The Cat was detected at two localities, near private properties of the Adams Estate Grantville and at Adams Creek NCR. The Red Fox was widespread, detected at all six localities. The most fox detections were on the Adams Estate, a settlement on the edge of the Nature Conservation Reserve. The deer species included Samba and Fallow Deer detected in the Adams Creek NCR and the Lang Lang Proving Ground, respectively. Unidentified species of deer were detected at these two localities.

Other species

There were 35 species of birds and three species of reptiles recorded. These species are listed in the Appendix in Table 3).

Most of the bird species were only briefly in front of the camera, so there were with just a very few images at each detection. The Eastern Whipbird is at the most western boundary of its distribution. The Bassian Thrush occurred in the wetter sites, notably at Wuchatsch Reserve, Nyora and at Lang Lang. The Grey Shrike-thrush was widespread. The Painted Button-quail occurred at five stations including the isolated Trust for Nature Granville property.

The Lace Monitor, the large lizard, is a top predator. It was detected at one station at each of the following bushland fragments; Adams Creek NCR, Gurdies NCR, Gurdies Shire Reserve, and the Trust for Nature Property at Grantville (east of Grantville NCR).

Special interest species

A timeline of the species of special interest, the endangered species, its two predators and a competitor, showed the typical pattern of detections over time, Figure 6. The results, here, are from an eastern portion of the Adams Creek NCR, the rail easement south of the Lang Lang Holcim Quarry.

The Red Fox was detected at all but one of these stations and generally on multiple days. Foxes commonly exploit roads and tracks to move through thick vegetation and the rail easement provides such access into the bushland from the farmland and the quarry. The European Rabbit was detected at only one of the stations, a station close to the farmland. The Southern Brown Bandicoot was detected at two stations (extending the known distribution, though this species has been regularly detected in nearby land parcels that make up the Adams Creek NCR, Nicholls pers comm., Ecology and Heritage Partners reports to Parks Victoria, Brooke pers comm.). The pattern of detections over the three months surveyed is similar to pattern observed elsewhere (Nicholls et al. 2018), namely detections over a few days and then absences for weeks or months. In this set of stations, the bandicoot occurred in the absence of the fox, but elsewhere in the greater study area both species occur at the same stations. The Cat was not detected in this set of stations. The full set of observations for these species are listed, Table 5.

This view of the data illustrates some characteristics of these camera trapping data. There is great variability both over space and time. Even stations spatially close or stations over the same time period often show very different patterns. Further, although a species is detected at a station, the pattern can range between detection on most days to just a single occasion.

Some of this variation was expected. The vegetation, especially in the native vegetation habitats, often changes markedly over tens of meters, a distance small relative to the home range of most of the mammals being studies. The camera is only recording over, at most, a few square metres. This is a tiny fraction of the home range of these species.

Vegetation and habitat

Ecological Vegetation Classes were mapped at a scale of 1:25000 (DELWP). At larger scales (smaller areas) there is much local variation in the understorey and ground level plants. For example in a mapped portion of Swamp Riparian Woodland north of the Lang Lang Proving Ground, we assessed the vegetation at nine camera stations recording two EVCs as lowland forest with Eucalyptus obliqua and damp heathy woodland with Eucalyptus radiata. The proportion of ground cover varied as did the species of plants. There are relatively few records of bandicoot to determine relationships, if any, between its distribution and the plants, indicating a need for additional surveys of the bandicoot’s distribution. Some habitats are illustrated, Figure 1, Figure 4, Figure 9, and Figure 10. The tree cover, the shrub and ground layers all differ substantially.

The vegetation was recorded at some stations. In all, over 290 assessments were made of the EVC and/or the floristics at 229 stations. Some stations were assessed twice, once at the deployment and again at the recovery. These records need careful editing to give a consistent name for the plant species. The structure of the vegetation was assessed in the Ecology and Heritage Partners work; it would be valuable to examine these two sets of observations, if only to better refine our understanding.

Each camera records temperature and a preliminary inspection of these observations showed a wide range in maximum and minimum temperatures between cameras deployed in the same bushland block over the same period. Some of these differences appeared to coincide with differences in vegetation with open sites recording a greater range of temperature.

Discussion

This project provides an important baseline of the current status of ground dwelling mammals in remnant patches of heathy woodlands and lowland forests in the Bass Coast hinterland. This habitat has been extensively cleared both locally and across the coastline and hinterland of Victoria (Logan et al. 2021). While the larger mammals were present in the survey area, small to medium-sized species such as Antechinus sp., native rodents and the two species of bandicoots appear restricted and uncommon. This is consistent with other local studies (Parks Victoria, Brook Love pers comm.) and more broadly across the state.

Southern Brown Bandicoot

The Southern Brown Bandicoot has been recorded infrequently in the study area since 1968. Recent surveys from 2011 to 2021 added new records in Adams Creek NCR (Nicholls, pers comm., Ecology & Heritage Partners, 2021) and this study has added further records in adjacent land parcels, Figure 7 and Table 5. This study complements surveys by Ecology and Heritage Partners in 2021 to give coverage across the entire Adams Creek block (B Love Parks Vic pers comm.). In the map of the historic records, two are located at the centre of the 5’ lat-log grids reflecting the imprecise location of the original records. This study recorded new records from the western and eastern parcels of the Adams Creek NCR. This species was not recorded south of Adams Creek NCR, Figure 8.

The Southern Brown Bandicoot has been recorded in the Wuchatsch Reserve and as a roadkill on Cherrytree Road but the species was not detected in this locality during the current study. It was also previously recorded in or near The Gurdies NCR and Grantville NCR (1968-1981, 1994). There is a possibility that the species may still occur in several small public land reserves and the private properties in the region, but these were under-represented in this study due to difficulties in gaining access.

This survey detected a bandicoot in Lang Lang Proving Ground but the images did not include sufficient diagnostic features so we could not determine the species with certainty. Given the Southern Brown has not previously been recorded at Lang Lang a parsimonious view would consider the observation as most likely to be of a Long-nosed Bandicoot. Further work is needed to confirm this.

The habitats in the study area were considered suitable for this species. The predominant Ecological Vegetation Classes (EVCs) for the northern study area are Heathy Woodlands, Herb-rich Foothill Forest and Riparian Scrub. For the Lang Lang area the classes are Lowland Forest, Heathy Woodlands, Swampy Riparian Woodlands, Herb-rich Foothill Forest. For The Gurdies, the classes are Lowland Forests, Heathy Woodlands, Herb-rich Foothill Forest. For Grantville, the classes are Damp Heathy Woodland, Heathy Woodland, Herb-rich Foothill Forest. These EVCs all support the dense ground vegetation crucial to the species’ persistence and are known to be occupied and indeed favoured by SBB elsewhere, (Coates et al, 2008). It is therefore a concern that that the species was not detected south of the South Gippsland Highway.

Despite the introduced predator, the Red Fox being common across the study area and around Western Port (Nicholls et al. 2018), bandicoots persisted in some localities. However, small, isolated populations are vulnerable to predation driven local extirpation, particularly following fire or other disturbance. A trans-location program could help re-establish populations in now unoccupied patches of vegetation and could help mitigate the reduced dispersal opportunities that stem from anthropogenic vegetation clearing.

Long-nosed Bandicoot

This survey confirmed recent records, from 2012 and 2019-2022 for the occurrence of the Long-nosed Bandicoot in the Lang Lang Education Area and the Lang Lang Proving Ground (VBA, Bass Coast Landcare Network pers comm., B Love Parks Victoria pers comm.). This survey also detected the species in the neighbouring private property, Flanders Field. There is one older VBA record from 1970, located south of The Gurdies NCR, in the Dandy Premix Quarry property.

Long-nosed Bandicoot typically occur in rainforest, moist gullies, grassy woodlands and heaths. It uses a mosaic of mixed habitats, including open grassy areas feeding in the grassy areas and sheltering in the understory of adjacent areas. While LNB are thought to be sympatric with Southern Brown Bandicoot in heath or heathy woodlands like those in the study area (Menkhorst 1995) we did not find sites where the two species occurred together. However, the distribution of the species in Victoria suggests a general preference for wetter and grassy habits compared to the Southern Brown Bandicoot. It is not clear that the vegetation of central region of the Bass Coast study area is substantially different from that of other parts. A habitat distribution model is the preferred tool to identify and quantify such a separation or preference of habitat. This is a key task for the future.

Other species

The invasive, introduced predator the Red Fox was repeatedly detected throughout the deployments and across the study area. No cats were detected. The Lace Monitor is known in the region but is only occasionally sighted today so detection at eight stations is of interest, see Appendix Table Table 4). The Mountain Brushtail Possum was observed, as expected in the wetter sites, but the images of the Common Brushtail Possums are to be checked to determine if both the Common and Mountain species are present and if so where.

Vegetation and habitat

Ecological principles suggest that the ground and understorey reflect the soil type and microclimate and consequently the quality of the shelter and the food resources utilised by the bandicoot (Claridge et al 2019). This could be a profitable field of study to understand the bandicoot’s local distribution. However, there are relative few records of bandicoot to determine relationships, if any, between its distribution and the vegetation, indicating a need for additional surveys of the bandicoot’s distribution. Our results provide a baseline from which to plan future studies. The Southern Brown Bandicoot occupies a wide range of understorey habitats where a common factor is not the floristics but rather the vegetation structure. Dense cover at ground level is very commonly independent of the floristics. However moist soils and loose or sandy soils often coincide with occupancy so the relationship between floristics and occupancy may not be strong.

Recommendations

  1. This region of the Bass Coast has a now rare combination of large fragments of native vegetation, several in public ownership in nature conservation reserves with smaller fragments also in public conservation reserves. And, it also has many private properties with high-quality bushland. This combination provides a special opportunity to maintain and reconnect this landscape. Such action could reverse the species loss and enhance biodiversity. Although many ground-dwelling mammal species may be at a lower abundance than the observations from earlier years much of the original fauna can still be found in the study area. Given that the Gippsland Plain has been extensively cleared, these bushland fragments make an extremely valuable contribution to ensuring the ongoing persistence of native mammals in the region. This is especially so given climate change and the wildfires that have occurred in all the large national park reserves; Grampians Gariwerd NP (three fires in a decade), Bunyip State Park and State Forest (2019), Wilsons Promontory National Park (several fires in the bandicoot habitat), and the East Gippsland national parks and state forest (2019-2020 extensive and severe fires). The Bass Coast NCRs and private properties in a rural landscape likely have a lower risk of extreme and extensive fires, thus enhancing their value to complement the large national parks for biodiversity conservation. These reserves and the privately owned bushland warrant the same protection and conservation management as the National Parks.

  2. One of the reasons there is so much native vegetation remaining is that is has sandy soils, soils that were not cleared for agriculture. However, these sandy soils are highly valued as earth resources and properties were purchased for this purpose in the 1980s. Today, several of these properties are being cleared and are a serious major threat to the environmental values by reducing habitat and disconnecting the landscape. This Bass Coast Bushland cannot be replaced. There are, however, alternative sources of earth resources on cleared land locally and beyond the region. In these circumstances, it is recommended that no further clearing occurs.

  3. There is existing state legislation that could be applied to strengthen the protection of the biodiversity values, since these woodlands require recognition and strong protection. The state government or the Bass Coast Shire Council should consider the following:

    • Apply Schedule 3 to Clause 42.01 Environmental Significance Overlay (Significant Flora and Fauna Habitats) to the remaining large native vegetation remnants on private land where a site contains vegetation types classified as depleted, rare or threatened at the state-wide level.

    • Remove Extractive Industry as a land use exempt from a permit in Table 42.02-3 in Clause 42.02 Vegetation Protection Overlay.

    • Apply Schedule 1 to Clause 42.02 Vegetation Protection Overlay (Significant Remnant Vegetation) to the remaining large native vegetation remnants, such that the Decision Guidelines in the Schedule apply, and any permit application is assessed against:

      • The effect on the habitat value and long-term viability of remnant vegetation in the locality

      • The significance of the vegetation, particularly if its forms part of a recognised wildlife corridor

      • The reason for any removal of any vegetation and whether an alternative option can be developed which conserved the vegetation

      • The effect of any removal of vegetation will have on groundwater recharge and discharge areas

      • The purpose of the underlying zone.

  4. The Southern Brown Bandicoot strategy appears to rely on dispersal in good seasons into locations where there is suitable habitat that can be colonised. The clearing of native vegetation fragments its habitat and restricts the likelihood of successful dispersal. Isolated patches are unlikely to be colonised or recolonised. The absence of this species from the larger nature conservation reserves south of the South Gippsland Highway is a concern that needs energetic conservation management. Ensuring that no further fragmentation occurs using planning control is necessary (see previous recommendation). Increased support is needed to create best practice wildlife corridors, also known as biolinks to reconnect this landscape. Recommendations (below) suggest tools that can quantify the costs and benefits. Support for the Save Western Port Woodlands and Bass Coast Landcare Network as they improve the retention of woodlands and woodland connections across this landscape is encouraged. The specialised management by Parks Victoria of the Adams Creek NCR is strongly supported and should be extend to The Gurdies NCR and the Grantville NCR.

  5. The results of this survey and those of Ecology and Heritage Partners highlight the potential importance of future trans-locations of SBB within the south-central region of Victoria. Two related projects: i) the Gippsland Transport Environmental Projects – Pilot Program (GTEP Pilot Program) to develop a trans-location strategy for the Bass Coast region and ii) DELWPs SBB genetic rescue project are timely and should be supported.

  6. The distribution of the two species of bandicoot, the Southern Brown and Long-nosed in this region is not clearly defined. While the current study had increased resolution on the distributions it is not clear if these species are sympatric in the study area or occupy different habitats. Further surveys to increase the number of known locations together with measures of the vegetation floristics and structure are required to be incorporated into species distribution models. The Biodiversity Climate Change Virtual Laboratory (BCCVL, https://bccvl.org.au/) has been used but this facility has closed. The BCCVL functionality to run Species Distribution and Climate Projections experiments will move to the EcoCommons Australia platform (https://www.ecocommons.org.au/) and will be made available to the research community on November 29th 2022. Results from the Species Distribution Model produced by this tool will be a valuable contribution to the conservation management of these two species.

  7. Increased fox control efforts are required. The work by Parks Victoria to monitor and control introduced predators in two reserves in the region has produced mixed results. The results suggest that the reduction in fox numbers is not marked and there is little indication that the Southern Brown Bandicoot numbers have greatly increased or expanded their range. An independent review of this programme using this survey’s results might improving the design of future fox control. Surveys confirmed and extended the known distribution of the Southern Brown Bandicoot in the north of the study area. The Ecology and Heritage Partners camera trapping included assessments of the vegetation at the camera sites. It is highly desirable that these data are analysed included in the SDM proposed above. An analysis using occupancy theory using all the available data is required as in Fiske and Chandler (2011).

  8. A population viability analysis that evaluates the existing, past, likely, and potential distributions is essential to the understanding for future conservation management. This should be supported with a study using GIS wildlife connectivity tools like “Circuitscape” or Makurhini: Analyzing landscape connectivity. These will provide objective criteria on how this landscape should be connected, either by maintaining and restoring connections with wildlife corridors and/or trans-locations.

  9. There is increasing evidence that wildlife underpasses are being used by Southern Brown Bandicoots and other fauna (Coates unpublished). Vic Roads installed such underpasses along Bass Highway. These should be monitored to provide insights on their effectiveness. As other roads are upgraded consideration should be given to providing this mitigation and other techniques to increase native mammal dispersal and reduce roadkill.

  10. The work of Ecology and Heritage Partners defined a rectilinear grid which was used to determine the locations for the cameras. This standard was followed in the larger bush blocks. However, limited resources meant that some bushland blocks were not completely surveyed. The shortcomings were in the western part of Grantville NCR, southern boundary of The Gurdies NCR, Lang Lang Proving Ground and Flanders Field. The smaller public land reserves were not surveyed. It is labour intensive to contact private property owners and managers and this is the reason that surveys on private property are under-represented, however, renewed interest and new contacts give opportunity. It would be valuable to survey these reserves and the private properties to make good the deficiency.
